0 · Last-time recap 3 min

Student sees a summary from last session_log + shaky chunks. First lesson → welcome message.

Teacher note: Read the summary aloud with student. Ask "what stuck with you from that?" before starting.

1 · Snowflake cards 10 min

TB Comm Activity 1.1 pattern. 12 topic cards. Pick 3, talk 90 sec each.

Teacher note (ICQ): "How many will you pick?" (3) · "Which grammar do we use?" (tend to / used to / would). Model the first with your own answer.
Model answer: "When I was a teenager, I used to be glued to the radio. I'd tape songs off Radio 1, and I hardly ever missed the top-40 countdown. Now, as a rule, I only listen to music while running."
Extension (stronger): ask student to also link chunks between two topics ("my teenage idol" + "posters on my wall").
Fallback (weaker): pick 2 cards instead of 3, give them 3 model chunks to hit.

2 · Swipe left / right 10 min

10 posters. Left = "not my thing", right = "would watch". One sentence with a chunk per swipe.

Teacher note (CCQ): "If I say 'It's a bit weird', is that praise or complaint?" (context-dependent — the whole point of chunks).
Model swipes:
  • Green Book → "It's a genuinely uplifting story — the plot revolves around an unlikely friendship."
  • Marvel Sequel → "Way too commercial for me. Over-the-top acting from the first minute."
  • Absurd Reality Show → "Not really my kind of thing — a bit weird and OTT."

3 · Bad-review rescue 12 min

Rewrite bland reviews using extreme adjectives with really/absolutely.

Teacher note: The whole point is the "very hilarious" bug. Elicit the rule from student: "With hilarious / dreadful / astonishing / gripping, can we say 'very'?" → No. → "Why not?" → Extreme adjectives are already at the maximum.
Answer keys:
  1. "The film was absolutely dreadful — I couldn't finish it."
  2. "Her stand-up is absolutely hilarious. I was in tears."
  3. "The final episode was genuinely gripping. I didn't move for 50 minutes."

4 · Preposition collision 8 min

12 sentences with fixed-preposition chunks. Not "rules" — pattern-noticing.

Teacher note: Do NOT teach these as "preposition rules". After student checks, ask: "Which of these did you get from memory? Which felt like guessing?" Guessed ones go to Leitner box 1.
All answers: addicted to · reliant on · driven by · open to · revolves around · satire on · sequel to · touched on · depicted as · in the foreground of · close to · disturbed by.

5 · The Critic — emoji → review 12 min

Student sees emoji only, writes 3-sentence review with 2+ chunks.

Teacher note: The film-reveal is optional and playful. Focus feedback on chunks used and register — not on whether they "guessed the movie".
Model reviews:

😱💀🤯 → "The plot revolves around a family unravelling after a death. Deeply disturbing — I looked away twice. A gripping ending, but not for everyone."

🥱😩💤 → "Over-the-top from minute one. Every plot twist felt commercial and safe. An all-time low for the franchise."

😭❤️🌟 → "A genuinely uplifting story. The plot revolves around two men on a road trip. I burst into tears twice."

6 · Dubbing lab 12 min

Silent 30-sec clip. Student writes 4 dialogue lines, using 5+ chunks.

Teacher note: Recommended clips: Wikimedia public-domain shorts, or classic silent-film segments (Chaplin, Keaton). Update units/unit-01/data.json with a chosen clip URL. Keep it ≤ 30 sec so the writing task stays tight.
Extension: record student reading their dubbing aloud → saves to creative_submissions.content_audio_url.

7 · Guess the plot from 3 chunks 8 min

Given three chunks, invent a synopsis. Reveal a real film.

Answer: Parasite (2019, Bong Joon-ho) — matches all three chunks precisely.
Extension: student generates their own 3-chunk challenge for you.

8 · Streaming Profile psy-test 15 min

6 personal questions → typology reveal. Universal (everyone watches). Full text saved to submissions.

Teacher note: The typology is playful — treat it that way. Focus feedback on chunk-density and precision. Which question triggered the most natural chunk usage? That's their strong context.

9 · Gallery Curator 20 min

6 real paintings (Wikimedia). Pick 3, answer curator's questions, write 3-sentence wall label.

Teacher note: This is not an art-history test. Zero knowledge needed. If student says "I don't know art" — reply: "Perfect, describe what you literally see." Focus is on chunks (foreground / atmospheric / depicts as / open to interpretation) reacting to real visuals.
Model wall label for Hopper's Nighthawks:
"In the foreground, three figures sit under harsh light — atmospheric and deeply lonely. The scene is open to interpretation: is this a meeting, or an accidental gathering? Hopper depicts urban night-life as beautifully empty."
